The Infinitely Many Days of Christmas

If after "The Twelve Days of Christmas" the presents kept coming, would we end up with -1/12 presents?

Most of us know the song, but for those who don't, let me summarize it in a mathematician's style: "On the 1st day of Christmas my true love gave to me a partridge in a pear tree. Then, for 2 ≤ n ≤ 12: On the nth day of Christmas my true love gave to me the same things as on the previous day, plus n of something (very often a bird of some sort)."

So on the twelfth day of Christmas, how many Christmas presents did we get? Hopefully you'll agree that would be 1 + 2 + ... + 11 + 12. If you're a physicist, you've probably already taken out your calculator by now, but if you're a mathematician, you likely suspect there's a better way. You're right!

In elementary school, Karl Friedrich Gauss and his classmates were asked by their teacher to add up all the integers (whole numbers) from 1 to 100. The teacher, surely thinking he had bought himself a few hours, was astounded to find Gauss almost immediately find the answer: 5050! So how did he do this? The number we're looking for is 1 + 2 + ... + 99 + 100. Let's call it S for "sum". Let's do a bit of mathematical gymnastics. S might seem tedious to calculate, so let's calculate 2S instead. 2S is simply (100 + 99 + ... + 2 + 1) + (1 + 2 + ... + 99 + 100) We can rearrange this as (1 + 100) + (2 + 99) + ... + (99 + 2) + (100 + 1). Take a second to convince yourself that this is equal to 50×101, which in turn is equal to 5050!

Upon me was once in my undergraduate years imparted a piece of valuable advice: "be wise, generalize!" After all, how else could we apply this to our festive problem? So let's take what we've learned and make a theorem out of it. Instead of taking the sum of natural numbers from 1 to 100, let's take the sum of natural numbers from 1 to n. Let's replace 100 with n in our above equation and obtain 1 + 2 + ... + n = (n/2)(n + 1).

Finally, we can calculate how many presents we've received after the twelfth day of Christmas. Using the formula 1 + 2 + ... + n = (n/2)(n + 1) and replacing n with 12, we get a total of (12/2)(12 + 1) = 78. There you have it! On the twelvth day of Christmas my true love gave to me (12/2)(12 + 1) = 78 presents.

I could end this post here, but that would leave out the folly of the following claim: 1 + 2 + 3 + ... = -1/12. This has been claimed by such sources as the popular YouTube channel Numberphile. But I know what you're thinking: "Max, that's ridiculous. Clearly the sum should be infinite. How could a sum of positive numbers be negative? How could a sum of integers be a non-integer?" Your worries are valid. In fact, I agree with you. Let's destroy their reasoning together.

The argument starts with the claim that 1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + 1 - ... = ½. If your instinct is telling you this looks wrong, you're right! But let's give them the benefit of the doubt and look at the proof. I want you to spot the mistake. Let's call this sum X. Now let's compute 1 - X. We have

1 - X = 1 - (1 + 1 - 1 + 1 - ...)

It follows that

1 - X = X

Therefore X = ½. So did you find the mistake? Well here it is: We've been treating 1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + 1 - ... as a real number! We called it X and assumed we could perform arithmetic on it. We can't. A sum like this with infinitely many terms is called a series. Roughly speaking, we call a series convergent if its total is a real number. For instance, the series ½ + ¼ + ⅛ + ... converges to 1. Intuitively, we can think of this sum as one in which the total slowly gets closer to 1 as we add more terms. When a series is not convergent, it's said to be divergent. This is the case for 1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + 1 - ..., as no matter how many terms we add, the total just alternates between 0 and 1. That is, it never approaches a real number. Since the sum is not a real number, we can't just assume we can apply arithmetic opperations to it. In the Numberphile video, they commit the same mathematical crime with the series 1 - 2 + 3 - 4 + ....

1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + ..., as you may have noticed, does not approach a real number value. It continues to grow and grow. It is, in fact, divergent.

Now you're all set for the holidays. In case there's an awkward silence during your family's Christmas dinner, you'll have something to break it.
